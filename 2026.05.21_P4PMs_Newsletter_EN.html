🎯 INTRODUCTION

Dear Project Management Colleagues,

The kickoff meeting went well. Stakeholders were engaged, the whiteboard was full, everyone nodded at the agenda. Then you sat down to write the requirements document, and you realized: you have 14 pages of meeting notes, three conflicting definitions of "done," and a business owner who said "it should be intuitive" four times without ever specifying what that means.

Requirements gathering is where most projects are quietly damaged before a single line of code is written. Poor or incomplete requirements account for over 39% of project failures — not budget overruns, not technology choices, not team performance. The ambiguity that enters at the requirements phase compounds at every subsequent stage, and by the time it surfaces as a defect or a missed scope item, the cost of fixing it is 10 to 100 times higher than catching it in discovery.

The structural problem: most PMs don't have a dedicated Business Analyst. In mid-size organizations and mixed-methodology projects, requirements elicitation, documentation, and validation fall to the PM — who was trained to manage delivery, not to write user stories in Gherkin format. AI changes this. Not by removing the PM's judgment, but by giving every PM BA-level output from PM-level inputs.

This issue gives you the exact workflow: from raw stakeholder notes to a structured user story backlog with acceptance criteria, a gap list, and a set of follow-up questions — all produced in under one hour of PM effort.

🔍 PRACTICAL EXAMPLE: STEP BY STEP

The Scenario: ERP Module Rollout, Three Business Units, No BA

Situation: A PM is leading the implementation of a new procurement module in an ERP system for a mid-size manufacturing company. Three business units are involved: Purchasing, Finance, and Warehouse. A 2-hour requirements workshop produced 14 pages of notes. There is no dedicated Business Analyst. The PM has 3 days to produce a requirements backlog before the vendor's development sprint planning.

The challenge: - Notes contain stakeholder opinions, process descriptions, IT constraints, and wish-list items — all mixed together with no structure - "The system should be fast" and "we need full visibility" appear repeatedly without measurable definition - The Finance lead and the Purchasing lead have contradictory expectations about approval workflow authority

Step 1: Extract and categorize raw requirements from meeting notes

The first prompt takes the raw meeting notes and turns them into a structured requirement inventory. The PM pastes the notes directly — no pre-processing required.

You are a Business Analyst helping a PM extract and categorize requirements
from a stakeholder workshop.

Raw meeting notes:
[PASTE YOUR FULL MEETING NOTES HERE]

Project context:
- System: [e.g., "SAP procurement module"]
- Stakeholder groups present: [e.g., "Purchasing, Finance, Warehouse — 12 participants"]
- Project phase: [e.g., "Requirements discovery, pre-development sprint planning"]

Extract and categorize all requirements into:

1. FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS: specific capabilities the system must perform
   - Format: "The system shall [action] for [user/role] when [condition]"
   - Remove duplicates; merge similar items
   - Mark confidence level: Clear / Needs clarification / Contradicts another requirement

2. NON-FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS: performance, security, usability, compliance constraints
   - Format: "[Category]: [measurable criterion]"
   - Include only items with enough specificity to be testable

3. ASSUMPTIONS: statements made during the workshop that were accepted as true
   but were not formally agreed upon

4. OUT OF SCOPE: items explicitly excluded OR items that appear to be out of scope
   based on the project context (flag these for PM review)

Output: a structured table for each category. Under 400 words per category.

What you get: A structured requirement inventory in 15 minutes that replaces 3 hours of manual note processing — with contradictions and unclear items already flagged for follow-up.

Step 2: Detect gaps, contradictions, and missing personas

Before writing a single user story, the PM runs a gap analysis on the extracted requirements. This is the step most PMs skip — and where the most expensive problems originate.

You are a requirements quality reviewer helping a PM identify gaps,
contradictions, and missing coverage before development begins.

Functional requirements:
[PASTE THE FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS FROM STEP 1]

Project context:
- System: [system name]
- Known user personas: [e.g., "Purchasing Agent, Finance Approver, Warehouse Receiver, IT Admin"]
- Non-negotiable constraints: [e.g., "SAP IS-U compliance, GDPR, approval limits per Finance policy"]

Perform a requirements quality review:

1. CONTRADICTIONS: list any two requirements that cannot both be satisfied as written.
   For each: state the conflict precisely and suggest the clarifying question
   that resolves it.

2. PERSONA GAPS: for each known persona, list any core workflow that has no
   corresponding requirement. A persona with no requirement for their most
   common action is a missing requirement, not an assumption.

3. AMBIGUOUS REQUIREMENTS: flag any requirement that uses vague language
   ("fast," "easy," "visible," "intuitive," "flexible") without a measurable
   definition. For each: suggest a specific, testable alternative phrasing.

4. MISSING EDGE CASES: identify 3–5 likely edge cases not addressed in the
   requirements — system behavior when an approver is unavailable, error handling
   for duplicate submissions, behavior under load, etc.

5. COMPLIANCE GAPS: flag any requirement area where regulatory or policy
   constraints likely apply but are not explicitly stated.

Output: a gap register table (Gap | Type | Risk Level | Clarifying Question).
Under 300 words total.

What you get: A gap register that surfaces the 5–10 issues that would otherwise surface as defects, change requests, or scope disputes during development — produced before a single story is written.

Step 3: Generate INVEST-compliant user stories

With a clean requirement list and gaps flagged, the PM generates the user story backlog. The AI formats each story to the INVEST standard (Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable) — the standard that makes stories actually usable in sprint planning.

You are an Agile BA helping a PM generate INVEST-compliant user stories
from a list of functional requirements.

Functional requirements (post-gap-review):
[PASTE YOUR CLEANED FUNCTIONAL REQUIREMENTS]

User personas:
[PASTE YOUR PERSONA LIST]

For each functional requirement, generate a user story:

FORMAT:
**Story ID:** [REQ-01, REQ-02, etc.]
**Title:** [short, action-oriented]
**User Story:** As a [persona], I want to [action], so that [business outcome].
**INVEST check:**
- Independent: [yes / partial — explain dependency]
- Estimable: [yes / needs spike — explain]
- Small: [yes / needs decomposition — suggest split]
**Priority suggestion:** [Must Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have]
**Story Points estimate:** [1 / 2 / 3 / 5 / 8 — based on complexity, not time]
**Open questions:** [any remaining ambiguity that affects estimation]

Flag any requirement that cannot be expressed as a single user story and suggest
how to decompose it into 2–3 smaller stories.

Output: one formatted story block per requirement.

What you get: A sprint-ready user story backlog in 15–20 minutes — formatted, prioritized, and flagged for any stories that need decomposition before they enter planning.

Step 4: Write Gherkin acceptance criteria

For each user story, the PM generates acceptance criteria in Gherkin (Given/When/Then) format. This step is the most frequently skipped in practice — and the one that prevents the most rework.

You are a QA-aware BA helping a PM write Gherkin-format acceptance criteria
for a set of user stories.

User stories:
[PASTE YOUR USER STORIES FROM STEP 3]

For each story, write acceptance criteria in Gherkin format:

SCENARIO: [scenario name — what situation is being tested]
  GIVEN [the system/user state before the action]
  WHEN [the specific action the user takes]
  THEN [the expected system response or outcome]
  AND [any secondary outcome or constraint to verify]

Rules:
- Write 2–4 scenarios per story: the happy path + at least one failure/edge case
- Each scenario must be independently testable without reference to other scenarios
- Use concrete values where possible (e.g., "GIVEN the order value exceeds €5,000"
  not "GIVEN a high-value order")
- Flag any scenario where the expected behavior is ambiguous based on the
  requirements — add a note rather than inventing the answer

Output: one Gherkin block per story. Include a summary line after each block:
"Testable: Yes / Needs clarification on: [specific open item]"

What you get: Ready-to-review acceptance criteria that the QA team can use directly for test case generation — and that the development team can use to build without back-and-forth clarification calls.

Step 5: Generate stakeholder follow-up questions

The gap register from Step 2 becomes a structured questionnaire for the next stakeholder meeting. AI organizes the questions by persona and priority so the PM walks in with a focused agenda, not a list of vague items.

You are a requirements facilitator helping a PM prepare targeted follow-up
questions for a stakeholder clarification session.

Gap register from requirements review:
[PASTE YOUR GAP REGISTER FROM STEP 2]

Stakeholder groups:
[PASTE YOUR STAKEHOLDER LIST WITH ROLES]

Generate a structured follow-up question set:

For each gap in the register:
1. Assign to the stakeholder group best positioned to answer it
2. Rephrase the gap as a direct, specific question — binary where possible
   (yes/no or option A vs. option B), open-ended only when the answer space
   is genuinely unknown
3. Estimate the decision impact: HIGH (blocks user story sign-off) /
   MEDIUM (affects acceptance criteria) / LOW (nice to clarify but not blocking)

Format as a meeting agenda:

**[Stakeholder Group Name] — [N] questions, [N] HIGH priority**
1. [Question] — Impact: HIGH/MEDIUM/LOW
2. [Question] — ...

Recommended time per group: [X] minutes
Total session time needed: [X] minutes

Output: a structured agenda the PM can send to stakeholders before the
clarification session. Under 300 words.

Result: A complete requirements package — requirement inventory, gap register, user story backlog with INVEST validation, Gherkin acceptance criteria, and a structured follow-up agenda — produced in under 90 minutes of PM effort. The PM walks into sprint planning with artifacts, not intentions.

📚 COOKBOOK: Requirements Gathering for PMs

Recipe #1: The Vague Language Extractor

What's the problem: Requirements documents are full of unmeasurable language — "the system should be fast," "the UI must be intuitive," "we need full visibility." These phrases pass review and become defects in UAT when the developer's definition of "fast" differs from the business owner's.

Prompt:

You are a requirements quality specialist helping a PM identify and remediate
vague, unmeasurable language in a requirements document.

Requirements document:
[PASTE YOUR REQUIREMENTS OR USER STORIES]

1. Extract every instance of vague, unmeasurable, or subjective language
   (e.g., "fast," "easy," "intuitive," "user-friendly," "scalable," "flexible,"
   "visible," "seamless," "robust," "real-time").

2. For each instance:
   - Quote the original requirement
   - Explain why it is not testable as written
   - Provide 2 specific, measurable alternatives
     (e.g., "page load under 2 seconds for 95% of requests" instead of "fast")

3. Identify the 3 most critical vague requirements — the ones most likely to
   cause a UAT dispute or scope change request if not clarified before development.

Output: a remediation table (Original | Why Not Testable | Alternative A |
Alternative B | Priority). Under 250 words.

How fast it works: 10 minutes → a list of all unmeasurable language with concrete alternatives Result: The PM catches the language that generates UAT disputes before the requirements are signed off — not during testing when fixing it costs 10× more.

Recipe #2: The Stakeholder Interview Prep Generator

What's the problem: The PM has 45 minutes with the CFO before the requirements workshop. Without targeted preparation, the meeting produces vague endorsements and general priorities — not the specific constraints and decision rules the project actually needs.

Prompt:

You are a requirements facilitator helping a PM prepare for a stakeholder
interview with a specific persona.

Stakeholder:
- Role: [e.g., "CFO — primary sponsor for the procurement module"]
- Known priorities: [e.g., "cost control, audit compliance, approval workflow visibility"]
- Known concerns: [e.g., "too many approval steps slow down operations"]
- Previous input: [e.g., paste any emails, comments, or prior meeting notes]

Project context:
- System: [system name and scope]
- Decisions this stakeholder owns: [e.g., "approval authority limits, exception workflow, budget thresholds"]

Generate a structured 45-minute interview guide:
1. Opening context-setter: 2-sentence framing to align the stakeholder on the
   purpose of this session (not the project — they know that; the purpose
   of this specific conversation)
2. 6–8 targeted questions, organized by decision area
   - Binary or multiple-choice where possible ("Should approval limits be role-based
     or individual?" not "How should approvals work?")
   - Include a "what would make this a failure?" question — the constraint the
     PM won't discover otherwise
3. One closing question: "What have we not discussed that you would regret not
   raising before development starts?"
4. 3 red flags to listen for — signals that the stakeholder's stated priorities
   may conflict with the documented requirements

Output: a printable interview guide the PM can use in the meeting.
Under 350 words.

How fast it works: 15 minutes → a focused interview guide that extracts the decisions and constraints the PM actually needs Result: A 45-minute stakeholder session produces specific, actionable input instead of general encouragement — the PM leaves with decisions, not impressions.

Recipe #3: The Epic Decomposer

What's the problem: The product owner writes epics. The development team needs stories. The PM is in the middle, and "implement the full procurement workflow" is not a story anyone can estimate or deliver in a sprint.

Prompt:

You are an Agile coach helping a PM decompose a large epic into sprint-sized
user stories that meet the INVEST criteria.

Epic:
[PASTE THE FULL EPIC DESCRIPTION]

Context:
- Team velocity: [e.g., "25–30 story points per 2-week sprint"]
- Sprint duration: [e.g., "2 weeks"]
- Target: stories should be 1–5 story points each
- Constraints: [any technical dependencies, integration points, or
  non-functional requirements that affect decomposition]

Decompose this epic:
1. Identify the vertical slices — end-to-end user value increments that
   can be delivered and tested independently (not horizontal layers like
   "do the database part" or "do the UI part")
2. For each slice, generate a user story:
   - Standard format: As a [persona], I want [action], so that [outcome]
   - Story points estimate with rationale
   - Dependencies: any other story that must be delivered first
   - Definition of Done: the minimum for this story to be "done" — 3 bullets max
3. Identify any enabler stories (technical setup, infrastructure) that are
   not user-facing but must precede user-facing stories — flag these separately
4. Suggest a delivery sequence for a 3-sprint roadmap

Output: a story map table (Story ID | Title | Story | Points | Dependencies |
Sprint Target) + a 2-sentence sequencing rationale.

How fast it works: 15 minutes → a sprint-ready story breakdown from a single epic description Result: The PM walks into sprint planning with stories the team can estimate — not an epic that produces a 2-hour backlog refinement session with no output.

Recipe #4: The Requirements Sign-Off Checklist Generator

What's the problem: The PM sends requirements to the business owner for sign-off. The business owner approves without really reading them — then raises "new requirements" during UAT that were missing from the original document. The PM has no documented evidence of what was approved.

Prompt:

You are a requirements governance specialist helping a PM create a structured
sign-off checklist to accompany a requirements document.

Requirements document summary:
[PASTE A SUMMARY OF YOUR REQUIREMENTS — OR THE FULL DOCUMENT FOR SMALL PROJECTS]

Stakeholders required to sign off:
[LIST NAMES, ROLES, AND THEIR APPROVAL SCOPE — e.g., "CFO approves financial
workflow requirements; IT Lead approves technical constraints"]

Generate a sign-off checklist:
1. For each stakeholder, list the specific requirements sections they are
   approving — with a checkbox and a statement of what approval means
   (e.g., "By signing this section, [Name] confirms that the approval workflow
   described in REQ-12 through REQ-17 accurately reflects the business policy
   and that no additional approval rules exist that are not documented here.")
2. A list of 5–7 explicit "I confirm" statements the approver must read
   and acknowledge — not a signature on a document, but specific confirmations
3. An "open items" section: requirements that are still pending clarification
   and therefore explicitly excluded from this sign-off round — so the business
   owner cannot later claim these were approved
4. A change request trigger statement: the specific condition under which a
   signed requirement can be changed and the process to do so

Output: a one-page sign-off document the PM attaches to the requirements package.
Under 300 words.

How fast it works: 15 minutes → a governance-ready sign-off document that protects the PM from "but we never agreed to that" Result: The PM has documented evidence of exactly what was approved, by whom, and on which date — and a clear process for handling the "new requirements" that arrive during UAT.

💡 KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • Requirements failures are the most expensive and the most preventable. 39% of project failures trace to poor requirements — not technical debt, not team performance. The gap register from Step 2 costs 15 minutes to generate and prevents the change requests, UAT disputes, and scope conflicts that each cost 10–100× more to resolve after development starts.
  • The BA gap is real, and AI closes most of it. Most PMs don't have a dedicated Business Analyst and are not trained to write Gherkin acceptance criteria or run INVEST validation on a backlog. AI does not require PM expertise in BA methodology — it requires PM expertise in the business problem, which the PM already has. The methodology is supplied by the prompt.
  • Vague language is a requirements defect. "Fast," "intuitive," "full visibility" — every one of these is a defect waiting to surface in UAT. Recipe #1 takes 10 minutes and eliminates the language that generates the majority of acceptance testing disputes. Run it on every requirements document before sign-off.
  • Sign-off without specificity is not sign-off. A business owner who approves a requirements document they haven't fully read is a change request risk, not a project milestone. The Recipe #4 checklist makes approval an active confirmation, not a passive signature — and creates the governance artifact the PM needs when "new requirements" appear in month three.
  • Start with the gap register, not the user stories. If you take one action from this issue: run Step 2 on your current requirements document before writing a single story. The gaps and contradictions you find in 15 minutes are the scope disputes and defects you will spend weeks resolving if you find them later.
