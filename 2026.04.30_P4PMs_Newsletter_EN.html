🔍 PRACTICAL EXAMPLE: STEP BY STEP

The Scenario: SAP Integration Project, Week 6, Three Stakeholder Audiences

Situation: A PM is running an SAP integration for a manufacturing client. Week 6 of 24. Three stakeholder groups: the client's steering committee (executive — wants RAG status and strategic risk), the internal technical team (needs blocker detail and dependency status), and the PM's own manager at the consulting firm (wants time and budget variance). Currently writing three separate reports every Friday afternoon, taking approximately 3.5 hours.

The challenge: - The same week's data needs different framing for three audiences — at different detail levels and with different risk tolerances - Blockers must be reported factually to the technical team but diplomatically to the client executive - The PM is the only person who can synthesize it — or so it seems

Step 1: Build the raw data input block

Before any AI prompt, the PM assembles the week's raw data into one structured input. This takes 10 minutes — not because AI requires formatted input, but because assembling the block forces the PM to actually think through what happened this week. Everything else is generated from this single source of truth.

WEEKLY STATUS INPUT — Week of [DATE]

PROJECT: [Project name and phase]
SPRINT/CYCLE: [Sprint number or week number]
ORIGINAL BASELINE: [Key milestones and dates from project plan]
CURRENT FORECAST: [Updated delivery forecast — if changed, note why]

COMPLETED THIS WEEK:
- [Task/deliverable 1 — who, what, status]
- [Task/deliverable 2 — who, what, status]

IN PROGRESS:
- [Task 1 — % complete, owner, expected completion]
- [Task 2 — % complete, owner, expected completion]

BLOCKERS AND RISKS:
- [Blocker 1: what it is, who owns it, what action is needed, what it affects]
- [Risk 1: what it is, probability, impact, current mitigation]

DECISIONS NEEDED FROM STAKEHOLDERS:
- [Decision 1: what needs to be decided, by whom, by when, consequence of delay]

NEXT WEEK PLAN:
- [Priority 1]
- [Priority 2]

BUDGET: [Planned spend to date vs. actual]
SCHEDULE: [On track / X days ahead / X days behind on specific milestone]

What you get: A 10-minute investment that produces the single source of truth for all reports. Every subsequent step is generation, not authoring.

Step 2: Generate the executive / steering committee report

The first report is the hardest to write manually — it requires translating complex technical progress into business language, flagging the right risks at the right level, and staying concise. AI generates this version in under two minutes.

You are a senior project management consultant helping a PM prepare a
weekly status report for an executive steering committee.

Project context:
- Project type and client: [e.g., "SAP integration for a manufacturing
  client, Week 6 of 24, fixed-price contract"]
- Audience: executive steering committee — they care about schedule,
  budget, key risks, and decisions they need to make. They do not want
  technical detail. They want to know: are we on track, is anything
  threatening the outcome, and do they need to do anything?
- Tone: professional, direct, no jargon. Risks stated honestly but
  without alarm. Blockers framed as "requiring a decision" not "problems."

Raw status data for this week:
[PASTE YOUR WEEKLY STATUS INPUT BLOCK]

Generate a steering committee status report:
1. RAG status (1 line): Overall — Red / Amber / Green, with one sentence
   explaining why
2. This week: 3–5 bullet points of what was delivered — framed as
   business outcomes, not task completions (e.g., "Integration spec
   signed off by client IT — Phase 2 can proceed as planned" not
   "Spec document completed")
3. Schedule: one line — on track / variance — and if variant, one
   line on the recovery plan
4. Budget: one line — on track / variance
5. Top 3 risks: Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation | Owner —
   only risks the committee needs to be aware of
6. Decisions required: any decision the committee needs to make this
   week, with the consequence of delay clearly stated
7. Next two weeks: the 2–3 most important milestones approaching

Format: two pages maximum. Bullet points. No paragraphs except the
RAG summary line.

Step 3: Generate the technical team update

Same data, different audience. The technical team needs blocker details, dependency status, and specific action items — not diplomatic framing.

You are a project coordinator helping a PM prepare a weekly technical
status update for the project delivery team.

Project context:
- Audience: technical team — developers, infrastructure engineers,
  integration specialists. They need: specific blocker details, who
  owns what, what dependencies are live. They do not need executive
  framing or RAG status.
- Tone: direct, operational, action-oriented.

Raw status data for this week:
[PASTE YOUR WEEKLY STATUS INPUT BLOCK]

Generate a technical team weekly update:
1. Sprint/week summary: what was closed — ticket IDs if available,
   owner, actual vs. planned status
2. Active blockers: for each blocker — what it is, root cause,
   who owns resolution, what unblocks it, and by when
3. Dependencies: what is waiting on whom — inside and outside the team
4. Next week priorities: top 5 tasks per sub-team, in priority order
5. Open questions: technical questions needing an answer before work
   can proceed — who to ask, what the deadline is
6. Watch items: 1–2 things not yet blockers but trending that direction

Format: short, scannable. Use tables for blockers and dependencies.

Step 4: Generate the client-facing progress update

For the shared client portal or client PM contact. Progress framing, no internal team issues, professional tone.

You are a client communications specialist helping a PM write a
professional weekly project update for a client contact.

Project context:
- Audience: the client's project lead — not technical, not on the
  steering committee. They want to know progress is happening and
  their interests are protected.
- Tone: professional, positive where genuine, transparent about
  challenges without alarm. No internal team issues. Focus on
  client-visible progress and any actions the client needs to take.
- Format: short email-style update, under 300 words.

Raw status data for this week:
[PASTE YOUR WEEKLY STATUS INPUT BLOCK]

Generate a client-facing weekly update:
1. Progress this week: 2–3 sentences in business terms the client
   understands
2. Schedule: one honest line — clients respect transparency, not spin
3. What we need from you: any action the client needs to take —
   specific deadline and consequence of delay
4. Next week: what the client will see happen — deliverables they
   can expect or reviews they are part of
5. Standard closing

Do not include internal blockers, team issues, or budget variance
unless directly relevant to the client's obligations.

Step 5: Generate the internal manager summary

The PM's own manager wants time tracking, budget variance, and escalation flags — candid and brief.

You are a project reporting assistant helping a PM prepare a brief
internal summary for their direct manager at a consulting firm.

Context:
- Audience: the PM's manager — they care about client relationship
  health, budget and time variance, team issues, escalation needs,
  and whether this project is tracking to be profitable and
  referenceable.
- Tone: internal, candid. No client-facing polish. Flag the things
  the PM would say verbally but softens in formal reports.

Raw status data for this week:
[PASTE YOUR WEEKLY STATUS INPUT BLOCK]

Generate an internal manager summary:
1. Client relationship: one sentence — how is the client feeling
   this week and why
2. Schedule and budget: actual vs. planned, with variance if any
3. Top issue this week: what kept the PM up at night — what happened,
   what was done, what still needs to happen
4. Escalation needed (yes/no): if yes, what specifically does the
   manager need to do or decide
5. Team health: one line — morale, any personnel issues
6. PM confidence level: 1–5 scale and one honest sentence on the
   biggest uncertainty going into next week

Format: under 200 words. This is a hallway update in writing.

Result: Four audience-specific reports generated from one input block in under 10 minutes of AI runtime. The PM's remaining job: read, adjust where needed, and send. Total weekly reporting time drops from 3.5 hours to 20–35 minutes.

📚 COOKBOOK: Weekly Status Report Automation for PMs

Recipe #1: The "One RAG Line" Executive Summary

What's the problem: The steering committee wants RAG status — but a single dot without narrative is meaningless. The PM needs to explain why in two sentences, diplomatically, every single week.

Prompt:

You are a project management advisor helping a PM write a clear, honest
RAG status summary for an executive audience.

Project status this week:
[Describe the current situation — what is on track, what is at risk,
what changed since last week]

Write a RAG status entry:
1. Overall status: Red / Amber / Green
2. One-sentence explanation of why — factual, not alarming
3. One-sentence description of what is being done (if Amber or Red)
   or what is sustaining Green status

Rules:
- No jargon.
- Do not catastrophize Red. Do not minimize it either.
- The explanation should be something the PM would say to the
  sponsor in a 30-second conversation.

Output: 3 lines. Nothing else.

How fast it works: 2 minutes → a RAG summary that a sponsor actually reads and understands Result: Stakeholders know the real status and the action being taken — without the PM writing a paragraph essay about why things are slightly off track.

Recipe #2: The Meeting-Notes-to-Status-Report Converter

What's the problem: The weekly status report is a synthesis of 4–5 meetings. The PM has notes but no time to turn them into a coherent report before the deadline.

Prompt:

You are a project management assistant helping a PM convert raw meeting
notes into a structured weekly status summary.

Meeting notes from this week (paste all — unformatted is fine):
[PASTE ALL MEETING NOTES]

Project context:
- Project type and phase: [...]
- Key stakeholder groups: [...]
- Current critical risks or blockers: [...]

Extract and structure into:
1. Decisions made this week: what was decided, who decided it, any
   conditions or follow-up required
2. Actions assigned: action | owner | due date | context
3. Progress signals: what moved forward, what slipped, what is new
4. Risks or blockers surfaced: anything raised in meetings that is a
   risk or blocker — even if mentioned informally
5. Open items: things discussed but not resolved — needs follow-up

Output: structured weekly status summary, ready to use as the input
block for the full reporting workflow above.

How fast it works: 5 minutes → raw meeting notes become a structured status input, ready for the full workflow Result: The PM never has to re-read five sets of notes to write a report. The synthesis is done before the first report prompt runs.

Recipe #3: The Blocker Escalation Note

What's the problem: A blocker has been unresolved for two weeks and escalation is needed. Writing the note is politically delicate — too mild and nothing happens, too alarming and the client relationship suffers.

Prompt:

You are a senior project manager helping a colleague write a professional
escalation note about an unresolved project blocker.

Blocker context:
- What the blocker is: [describe — what is stuck and why]
- How long it has been open: [e.g., "10 business days"]
- What has been tried: [actions already taken to resolve it]
- Impact: [what is this blocking — specific deliverables, which
  milestone, delay if unresolved by end of week]
- Who needs to act: [who has the authority to unblock this]
- Tone required: [e.g., "firm but professional — good relationship
  with this person but this needs to get done"]

Write a brief escalation note (under 150 words):
1. One sentence stating the situation factually
2. One sentence on the business impact of continued delay
3. One specific ask — what decision or action is needed, by when
4. One sentence offering to discuss

Do not be passive-aggressive. Do not catastrophize. Be direct.

How fast it works: 3 minutes → a professional escalation note that says exactly what needs to be said Result: The PM sends the note instead of drafting and deleting it three times. Blockers get unblocked.

Recipe #4: The Milestone Log Entry

What's the problem: At the end of a major milestone, the PM needs to record what happened in the project log — not just what was delivered but what can be learned. This always gets skipped because there is no time.

Prompt:

You are a project documentation specialist helping a PM write a
milestone log entry for the project record.

Milestone context:
- What was delivered: [description of the milestone output]
- Original plan (scope, timeline, budget): [...]
- Actual outcome (scope, timeline, budget): [...]
- Top 2 things that went well: [brief description]
- Top 2 things that did not go as planned: [description + root cause]
- One thing the team would do differently: [...]

Write a milestone log entry:
1. Delivery summary: what was delivered, on/off track, variance if any
2. What worked: 2 bullet points — specific enough to be replicable
3. What to improve: 2 bullet points — root cause, not just symptom
4. Recommendation for Phase [N+1]: one actionable change to the
   approach for the next phase based on this milestone experience

Format: under 300 words. Written for a future reader who was not on
this project — clear, specific, no internal shorthand.

How fast it works: 10 minutes → a milestone entry that actually gets written instead of skipped Result: Project history is captured while knowledge is fresh — and the next phase starts with a documented lesson instead of a repeated mistake.

💡 KEY TAKEAWAYS

  • The Friday status problem is a data aggregation problem, not a writing problem. The PM already knows what happened this week. The time cost is in collecting, synthesizing, and reformatting for multiple audiences. AI collapses that cost to near zero — if you build one structured input block, everything else is generated.
  • One input, four reports. The workflow in this issue generates a steering committee report, a technical team update, a client-facing update, and an internal manager summary from a single 10-minute data input. The PM shifts from author to editor — a fundamentally different and much faster role.
  • 54% of PM time on admin tasks — status reporting is the biggest share. Even a 70% reduction — from 3.5 hours to 1 hour per week — returns 2.5 hours of PM capacity per project week. On a 6-month project, that is 60 hours recovered without adding headcount.
  • Diplomatic framing is a skill AI handles well. The hardest part of writing status reports is often not the content — it is knowing how to say "we are two weeks behind" to the client in a way that maintains the relationship. AI generates the diplomatically calibrated framing; the PM validates it. That is a much faster workflow than writing from scratch.
  • Start with one report. If the full workflow feels like too much at once, start with Recipe #2: paste this week's meeting notes into Claude and ask for a structured summary. Once you see the output quality, the rest of the workflow becomes obvious — and worth setting up.
